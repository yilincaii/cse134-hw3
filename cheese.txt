A Short Guide to Cheese

by The CSE Cheese Society. Last updated January 2026.

Cheese is one of humanity's oldest prepared foods. People have made it
for at least 7,000 years, long before written history. At its core,
cheese is simple: milk, salt, cultures, and rennet. Time and technique
do the rest.

What Is Cheese, Really?

Cheese begins when milk separates into curds and whey. The curds are
pressed, salted, and aged. Every variable matters: the animal, the
season, the cave, the patience of the maker. Change one and you get a
different cheese. This is why a single recipe has produced thousands
of distinct varieties.

The word "cheese" comes from the Latin caseus, which also gives us the
word "casein," the main milk protein.

Famous Families of Cheese

Fresh cheeses are young and mild. They include mozzarella, ricotta,
and chevre. Eat these within days.

Soft-ripened cheeses grow a white bloomy rind. Brie and Camembert are
the classic examples. The rind is edible and you should eat it.

Blue cheeses are veined with Penicillium roqueforti. Roquefort,
Gorgonzola, and Stilton lead this family. They are salty, sharp, and
divisive.

Hard cheeses are aged for months or years. Parmigiano-Reggiano,
aged Gouda, and Comte reward the wait with crystals of crunch and
deep, nutty flavor.

A Word of Caution

Warning: The American product known as "processed cheese food" is,
at best, distantly related to cheese. Read the label.

As the writer Clifton Fadiman put it, cheese is milk's leap toward
immortality.
